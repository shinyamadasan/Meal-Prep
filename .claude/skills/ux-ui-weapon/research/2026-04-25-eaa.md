**Date:** 2026-04-25

## Source B — AccessibleEU / European Commission

URL: <https://accessible-eu-centre.ec.europa.eu/content-corner/news/european-accessibility-act-enters-force-2025-06-27_en>
Published: 2025-06-27.

Authoritative confirmation: "the European Accessibility Act (EU Directive 2019/882) … As of 28 June, the emergency number 112, banks, public transport and other products and services must be accessible to the more than 440 million European citizens, especially the 100 million people with disabilities living in the EU."

Confirms scope:

- ATMs (multi-sensory accessible interfaces, height requirements).
- Online banking.
- Public transport (websites, mobile apps, info screens).
- Computers, smartphones, e-commerce, TVs, e-books, AV playback.
- Customer support training requirement.

## Source C — EU Perspectives

URL: <https://euperspectives.eu/2025/06/european-accessibility-act/>
Published: 2025-06-28.

Confirms the EAA was adopted in 2019 after "years of negotiation," and that "Enforcement falls to individual Member States, but the penalties can be steep: fines, withdrawal of non-compliant products or services, and liability for company officers."

Lists scope (matching Source B):

- Computers and operating systems
- ATMs, ticketing, check-in machines
- Smartphones
- TV equipment for digital TV services
- Telephony services and equipment
- Audio-visual media services + consumer equipment
- Air, bus, rail, waterborne passenger transport services
- Banking services
- E-books
- E-commerce

Practical: services must "make websites and mobile apps accessible, and to offer alternative ways to communicate, such as real-time text, sign language support, or accessible customer service interfaces."

## Source D — Heurilens compliance checklist

URL: <https://heurilens.com/blog/technical-ux/european-accessibility-act-eaa-2025-compliance-checklist>
Published: 2026-04-14 (recent).

Critical confirmations:

> On 28 June 2025, the European Accessibility Act (EAA) became directly enforceable across all 27 EU member states. For the first time, private-sector digital products and services — e-commerce storefronts, SaaS platforms, banking apps, e-readers, streaming services and more — must meet a harmonized accessibility standard or face fines, injunctions and market-access restrictions.

Microenterprise exemption confirmed:

> There is a limited microenterprise exemption for services: businesses with fewer than 10 employees and annual turnover or balance sheet under €2 million are exempt from the service requirements (but not product requirements). … And the exemption does not travel with your customers: an exempt vendor cannot shield a non-exempt client from their own compliance obligations.

US SaaS clarification:

> A US SaaS selling to EU consumers is a service provider and must comply directly.

Standard confirmation:

> For almost all digital services, the standard is WCAG 2.2 AA via EN 301 549. Document this decision.

## Source E — BTNG e-commerce guide

URL: <https://www.btng.studio/articles/european-accessibility-act-ecommerce-complete-compliance-guide-for-online-retailers>

Confirms: "10 or more employees: EAA applies. Annual turnover above €2 million: EAA applies. Fewer than 10 employees AND below €2 million turnover: exempt (for now)." (Either condition triggers full compliance — the exemption requires both to be false.)

Note: this source mentions "WCAG 2.1 AA" as the legal floor, while Source D (more recent) confirms 2.2 AA via EN 301 549. The Heurilens / 2026-published timing is more authoritative on the current standard. Either way, the Angel's `guides/11-wcag-2-2-baseline.md` covers the more recent floor.

## Source F — Tobalt practitioner write-up

URL: <https://tobalt.eu/european-accessibility-act-2025/>
Published: 2026-03-15.

Confirms scope of "e-commerce services":

> The phrase e-commerce services is particularly important. It covers services provided at a distance, through websites or mobile-based means, by electronic means and at the individual request of a consumer, with a view to concluding a consumer contract. This means many online shops, booking flows, subscription sign-up journeys and transactional portals may be in scope.

Reinforces that B2B-only is largely out of scope, but B2B2C surfaces still matter.

## Operational decisions made for the guide

1. **Position EAA as a "why" guide and 11 (WCAG 2.2) as the "what."** The Angel's review behavior changes only in tone (severity bumps, EAA-RISK tagging) — the technical floor is the same.
2. **Don't have the Angel certify legal exemption.** Microenterprise self-declaration is noted, but the technical floor still applies. Legal certification is out of scope.
3. **List the EAA-enumerated digital service surfaces (e-commerce, banking, transport, streaming, e-books) as severity-amplifiers.** A WCAG failure on a checkout flow is heavier than the same failure on a marketing page.
4. **Escalate to `design-system-guardian`** for: certified compliance audit, EN 301 549 (vs. WCAG 2.2 AA) verification, member-state-specific transposition, conformance-statement drafting.

## Open questions

- Should the Angel keep a per-member-state cheatsheet (German BFSG, French RGAA, Spanish UNE)? Defer — the Angel does the technical floor; member-state nuance is a legal-counsel question.
- Does the Angel need a separate EAA-RISK tag in `templates/review-output.md`? Yes — added to guide #12 spec; future template update can codify.
- Does the EAA cover internal admin tools? Generally no (B2B / internal), but if an internal tool has external consumer touch — e.g., a customer-support agent's tool that exposes content to the consumer side — that surface is in scope. Defer to legal.
